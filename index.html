Buy Borrow Die

Calculate your maximum debt-to-equity ratio and the number of years it takes to reach it using the Buy Borrow Die strategy.

This website is processed locally on your browser, no data is transmitted.

How Does This Work?

If you want a closer look at the math functions used in this tool, go to my Desmos Graph.

Say you have a starting equity balance of $1,000,000, then you have an expense of $50,000. Instead of selling $50,000 worth of your equity to pay for it, you elect to borrow $50,000.

You now have two balances: debt

d 0 = 50000

and equity

q 0 = 1000000

Equity

Your entire equity stays invested because you chose to borrow money to pay for your expenses. It grows according to your investments' rate of return, say 10%.

r = 1.1

After one year, your equity is $1,100,000. After two years it's $1,210,000. The function for your equity at year x is simply

q ( x ) = q 0 r x

Debt

Your debt starts out at $50,000. After one year, you are charged interest on your entire debt amount, say 6%.

z = 1.06

If your debt was $50,000, you are charged $3,000 of interest. Again, instead of selling your equity to pay for this expense, you elect to borrow more money! Your debt is now $53,000.

Except now you have another year of living expenses you need to pay for and the price of those expenses has gone up according to your inflation rate, say 3%.

w = 1.03

So instead of another $50,000 expense, your living expense after one year is $51,500. If you add it all up, your previous debt times your inflation rate plus your new expense, you get $104,500. The formula for debt at year n is

d n = z d n - 1 + w n d 0

Explicit Formula

One problem with our debt equation is that it requires the previous year's debt d n - 1 . We can find an explicit formula for debt like we have for equity. Let's write out the first few years of debt and see if we find a pattern. d 1 = z d 0 + w d 0

d 2 = z ( z d 0 + w d 0 ) + w d 0 = z 2 d 0 + z w d 0 + w 2 d 0

d 3 = z ( z 2 d 0 + z w d 0 + w 2 d 0 ) + w 3 d 0 d 3 = z 3 d 0 + z 2 w d 0 + z w 2 d 0 + w 3 d 0

A pattern is starting to appear. The power of the z in each term goes down starting from n to 0. Also, the power of w in each term goes from 0 to n . Finally, each term has a single d 0 in it. Let's factor that out and rewrite the equation with these patterns in mind.

d n = d 0 ( z n + z n - 1 w + z n - 2 w 2 + z n - 3 w 3 + … + w n )

Geometric Series

It turns out this equation is a type of geometric series. Each term has the same base, but the exponents change. Let's isolate the geometric series and call it S .

S = z n + z n - 1 w + z n - 2 w 2 + z n - 3 w 3 + … + w n

Watch what happens when we multiply S by z - 1 w

z - 1 w S = z n - 1 w + z n - 2 w 2 + z n - 3 w 3 + z n - 4 w 4 + … + z - 1 w n + 1

All the exponents changed, so what? Notice how this new series, is same as the old one minus z n and plus z - 1 w n + 1 .

z - 1 w S = S - z n + z - 1 w n + 1

Now solve for S .

z - 1 w S - S = - z n + z - 1 w n + 1 ( z - 1 w - 1 ) S = - z n + z - 1 w n + 1 S = - z n + z - 1 w n + 1 z - 1 w - 1 = z-1(-zn+1+wn+1)z-1(w-z) = -zn+1+wn+1w-z = zn+1-wn+1z-w

Finally, if we plug everything back in, we have a closed-form solution for our debt.

d(x)mrow>=d0zx+1-wx+1z-w

Debt-to-Equity Ratio

Now to find our debt-to-equity ratio at time x our function is simply debt over equity.

d(x)q(x)
